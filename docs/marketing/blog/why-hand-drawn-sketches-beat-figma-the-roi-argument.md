## The Hidden Cost of Polished Wireframes

There's a number your design tool vendor doesn't want you to calculate: the cost of fidelity.

Every time you open Figma for a quick idea, you spend 2-4 minutes on components, auto-layout, spacing, naming, and saving before you've communicated a single creative thought. If you do this 10 times a day, that's 20-40 minutes of **pure overhead**. Not design thinking. Not problem-solving. Just tool management.

Multiply that by your full product team. Add the rework when the idea was wrong anyway.

The ROI of the rough sketch isn't just about speed. It's about reducing the cost of being wrong.

---

## The "Expensive Opinion" Problem

Here's how most product teams work today:

1. Designer has an idea
2. Designer spends 2 hours making it look good in Figma
3. Designer presents it looking polished
4. Stakeholders feel awkward criticizing something that looks "done"
5. The mediocre idea ships

This is what researchers call the **commitment escalation trap**. The more polished something looks, the less likely people are to challenge it — even when they should.

Hand-drawn sketches sidestep this entirely. Nobody protects a sketch. Scratching out a box on paper (or a JSON-rendered sketch) invites improvement. It *looks* like it's meant to be changed.

**That's not a bug. It's the entire point.**

## The Adobe 30% Signal

Adobe's 2024 Creative Trends Report found a **30% rise in demand for hand-drawn, imperfect design assets**. This isn't nostalgia. It's a response to AI monoculture.

As generated content saturates every surface, roughness has become a signal. It says: *a human thought about this*. *This is one idea among many, not the final word.*

In 2026, rough sketches don't look amateurish. They look **deliberate**.

## What Figma Is Actually For

Figma is excellent at exactly one thing: communicating *decisions*.

When you know what you're building — when the thinking is done and you need to hand specifications to an engineer — Figma is the right tool. It gives you precision, components, handoff specs, annotations.

But Figma is bad at *finding* decisions. It's bad at the phase before you know what to build.

That's the napkin phase. The phase where you need to try 10 ideas in the time Figma takes to load one.

**Use the right tool for the phase you're in:**

| Phase | Question | Tool |
|-------|---------|------|
| Discovery | What should we build? | Sketches |
| Exploration | How might it work? | Sketches + whiteboard |
| Definition | What exactly are we building? | Figma |
| Delivery | How do we build it? | Figma + specs |

Most teams use Figma for phases 1 and 2. That's where the waste comes from.

## The AI-Native Case

There's a newer argument that Figma's DNA makes it poorly suited for: **AI collaboration**.

When you're working with Claude, ChatGPT, or any LLM to explore ideas, the conversation is fast and non-linear. You say "what if the dashboard led with the map instead of the list?" The AI responds with three variations. You explore. You backtrack.

Figma can't keep up with that cadence. It takes you out of the flow every 90 seconds.

Skissify was built for exactly this: you describe what you want (in JSON or plain text), you get a sketch URL back in under a second, you continue the conversation. The sketch is in the chat. The agent can reference it. You can modify it. It stays in the loop.

The sketch isn't the destination. It's a thinking artifact that lives inside the conversation.
