# Why Hand-Drawn Sketches Beat Figma: The Honesty Argument

*The insight nobody in design tools marketing is allowed to say out loud.*

---

**TL;DR:** Polished mockups make stakeholders think the work is done. Rough sketches make them think the work is open. One of those is true when you're still exploring.

---

## The Meeting Experiment

Here's something you can test yourself, for free, in the next week.

In your next design review, show a polished Figma mockup. Watch what happens. People comment on the color. They ask why you chose that font. Someone says "can we make the button a bit bigger?" Someone else says the spacing feels off.

The next week, show a hand-drawn sketch of the same concept — or a Skissify-rendered version, deliberately rough. Watch what happens differently. People ask about the flow. They question whether this is even the right screen. Someone says "wait, what happens when the user does X?" Someone else goes to the whiteboard.

One of those conversations is about execution details. The other is about whether you're building the right thing.

This is the honesty argument for sketches. Not that they look more artistic, or that they're faster to make (though they are), or that they "communicate the idea without over-committing." It's simpler: **rough signals incomplete, and incomplete invites honest feedback.**

---

## The Signal People Don't Say Out Loud

When a Figma mockup is in front of a stakeholder, they read the production quality as a signal of completion. The unconscious message is: "This is what we're building, we just need your sign-off."

Designers know that's not how they intended it. But the stakeholder's brain processes visual polish as a commitment signal. They're not trying to be difficult — their brain is literally interpreting the artifact the way brains interpret artifacts.

This is documented in design research. Studies on "fidelity signaling" (the idea that artifact quality signals intent and openness) consistently show that rough prototypes generate more substantive feedback than polished ones. Higher fidelity = lower feedback quality in the exploration phase.

The problem is that polished design tools have won the design workflow. Figma, Sketch, Framer — they're all optimized for the execution phase. They're incredible at execution. But they've colonized the exploration phase too, because designers use them for everything.

The result: exploration-phase meetings with execution-phase artifacts. Wrong tool, wrong signal, wrong conversations.

---

## What "Wrong Conversations" Cost

Let's put a number on it.

Assume you have one design review per sprint. Assume the review takes 2 hours with 6 people (designer, PM, tech lead, 3 stakeholders). Assume 30% of that meeting is spent on polish-level feedback that wouldn't exist if the artifact were rough.

That's 36 minutes per sprint. Per team. At €90/hour average loaded cost per person: **€324 per sprint in feedback-on-the-wrong-level**.

At 24 sprints per year, per team: **€7,776/year**.

With multiple design projects running in parallel: scale accordingly.

This isn't the "Figma Tax" of licensing costs. This is the cognitive overhead tax: decision-making at the wrong level, at the wrong time, because the artifact signaled the wrong thing.

## Where Skissify Fits

Skissify doesn't replace Figma. That's an important thing to be clear about.

Figma is for execution. For building design systems, handoff to developers, polished mockups for production-ready review. It's exceptional at that.

Skissify is for exploration. For the phase where you're still figuring out what you're building. Where you want honest feedback, not polish feedback. Where you want people to challenge the concept, not debate the button radius.

The workflow is: **Skissify first, Figma second**.

Use a sketch when you're asking "is this the right thing to build?"
Switch to Figma when you've answered that question and you're asking "how do we build it well?"

The transition point is usually when stakeholders stop saying "but what if we did X instead" and start saying "how will this work with the existing component library?"

## The AI-Native Advantage

There's one more reason sketches matter in 2026 specifically: AI agents don't natively produce Figma files.

When you ask an AI to help with design exploration, it produces text descriptions, JSON structures, code — not Figma files. The output format creates a friction gap: AI thinks in concepts, Figma demands execution-level precision.

Skissify closes that gap. Give an AI a JSON schema (or just describe what you want in plain text with Human Mode), and you get back a hand-drawn sketch. The AI can explore, iterate, and communicate visually — without the friction of execution-level tools.

The format matches the phase. That's the whole point.
