## The Fidelity Signal Effect

When you hand someone a polished mockup, they respond to it like a finished product. They nitpick colors. They ask about font choices. They wonder if the button should be outlined or filled.

When you hand them a rough sketch, they respond to it like an idea. They say "what if we moved this here?" They cross things out. They add a bubble and say "could we add this later?"

The same concept, communicated at different fidelity levels, produces fundamentally different feedback.

This is called the **fidelity signal effect**, and it's well-documented in UX research. Low-fidelity representations invite collaborative editing. High-fidelity representations invite critical evaluation. The format of the artifact changes the cognitive mode of the reviewer.

Figma is optimized for communicating decisions. Sketches are optimized for making them.

## The Blank Canvas Problem

Figma's biggest hidden cost isn't the subscription. It's the cognitive overhead of the infinite canvas.

When you open a blank Figma file, you face an existential design question before you've drawn a single pixel: *where do I start?* The tool offers no constraints. It has no opinion. Every configuration is equally valid.

Constraints are not creative limitations. They're creative fuel. The deadline that forces a decision. The paper napkin that limits you to what matters. The JSON schema that forces you to be explicit about what elements you actually need.

Skissify's JSON format forces a useful constraint: you must describe your idea in terms of its actual structural components. A room is a rectangle. A door is a door type, a wall, and a side. When you're forced to name things explicitly, you understand them better.

## The AI-Native Case

Here's the argument that seals it for 2026: AI agents are now primary authors of first drafts.

When Claude generates a floor plan, it's not a designer with taste making aesthetic choices. It's a language model predicting what JSON tokens best describe a spatial layout. The output should *look* like what it is — a structured first draft, not a finished rendering.

Skissify's hand-drawn aesthetic is honest. It says: "An AI made this. It might be wrong. Tell me what to change."

A photorealistic floor plan generated by AI says: "This is what your kitchen will look like." That's a lie at the draft stage.

The rough sketch is epistemically honest. And in a world where AI can generate convincing final-looking output, honest rough drafts are more valuable than ever.

## When Figma Wins (And You Should Use It)

Sketches aren't a Figma replacement. They're a Figma precursor.

Use a sketch (or Skissify) when:
- You're still figuring out what you're building
- You need fast feedback before committing to implementation
- You're working with non-designers who get intimidated by polished mockups
- You're asking an AI to generate options you'll evaluate later
- You need to communicate structure, not aesthetics

Use Figma when:
- The structure is decided and you're communicating final design
- You need interactive prototypes
- You're handing off to engineers who need exact specs
- You're building a design system component

The best design process flows through rough → refined → final. Skissify lives in the rough stage. It's not competing with Figma. It's what happens before Figma.
