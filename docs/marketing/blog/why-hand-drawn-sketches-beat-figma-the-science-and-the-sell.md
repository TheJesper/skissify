## The Fidelity Paradox

In UX research, there's a well-documented phenomenon: the more polished a prototype looks, the more people comment on surface-level details rather than structural problems.

A rough sketch sends a signal: *this is a draft, tell me if the idea is wrong.*  
A polished mockup sends a different signal: *this is nearly finished, spot the errors.*

Same concept. Same room layout. Completely different conversations.

This isn't a soft feeling. Studies on design critique consistently show that low-fidelity prototypes get more substantive structural feedback, while high-fidelity ones attract pixel-level comments. The aesthetic is a communication strategy.

---

## Where Figma Actually Belongs

Figma is excellent at one job: communicating *decisions that have already been made*.

It's a handoff tool. A documentation format. A precision instrument for when you know what you're building and need to tell someone else how to build it.

Figma is for the 80% of design work that happens after the idea is validated.

The rough sketch is for the 20% that comes first — the part where you're still finding out whether the idea works at all.

Using Figma for early exploration is like using a scalpel to chop vegetables. Technically possible. Wildly miscalibrated.

---

## The "Move That Wall" Test

Here's a thought experiment:

You're describing an apartment layout to a contractor. You have two options:

**Option A:** Open Figma, arrange pixel-perfect rectangles, export a PDF.  
**Option B:** Sketch on paper (or describe it in plain text and generate a rough sketch).

Option A takes 45 minutes. Option B takes 3.

In Option A, the contractor will ask which font you used in the room labels. In Option B, they'll say "that hallway feels too narrow — can we push the bedroom wall back two feet?"

That second conversation is the one that matters.

## Why AI Makes This Even More Relevant

Here's where it gets interesting for 2026.

AI agents are incredible at generating structured descriptions of spaces. Ask Claude to describe an apartment layout: you get dimensions, proportions, relationships between rooms, furniture placement.

What AI hasn't been able to do — until recently — is *show you* those descriptions visually, without requiring a human designer in the loop.

That's the gap we built Skissify to fill.

The rough sketch aesthetic isn't just aesthetically pleasing. It's *semantically correct* for what AI-generated spatial descriptions are: drafts. First attempts. "Here's what I understood — does this match what you meant?"

When AI agents produce hand-drawn sketches rather than pixel-perfect floor plans, they're communicating at the right fidelity level for the stage of the conversation.
