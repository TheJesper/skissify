# Why Your Product Team's First Two Hours Should Never Touch Figma

*~620 words | Category: Product Management | Tags: figma, sketching, product teams, ideation, design process*

---

There's a specific failure mode in product teams that nobody names, but everyone has experienced: the Figma meeting that was supposed to be a brainstorm.

Someone shares their screen. There's a wireframe. It's in Figma. It has frames and auto-layout and a couple of components from the design system. Someone asks "can we move this section?" and the designer starts resizing boxes in front of everyone on a 30-minute call.

This is not design collaboration. This is live editing with an audience. The conversation about *whether this is the right thing to build* has been replaced by a conversation about where to put the boxes you've already decided to build.

The problem isn't Figma. The problem is using Figma in the wrong phase.

---

## The Phase That Figma Breaks

Figma is optimized for production: precise layout, real components, engineer handoff. It is genuinely excellent at this.

It is actively harmful in the ideation phase — the first few hours when you're asking "what should this even be?" — because the pixel fidelity of a Figma mockup sends the wrong psychological signal.

Research from HCI conferences (Carnegie Mellon, CHI proceedings) has consistently shown that stakeholders give deeper structural feedback on rough, low-fidelity representations than on polished ones. The polish signals "decisions were already made." Roughness signals "everything is still on the table."

Your product team's biggest competitive advantage in early product development is the speed at which you can cycle through wrong ideas to find the right one. Figma slows this down. A rough sketch accelerates it.

---

## The Specific Workflow That Works

**First 60 minutes:** Whiteboard or rough sketches only. If the team is co-located, use a physical whiteboard. If remote, use Skissify — describe the idea to Claude in plain English, get a hand-drawn sketch in 20 seconds, share it in Slack. The point is to externalize the idea visually at zero cost.

**Minutes 60–90:** Pick the two or three ideas worth refining. Clean them up just enough to be specific — label key areas, indicate proportions, mark what's interactive. Share async for feedback. Goal: everyone agrees on the direction before anyone builds a component.

**After alignment:** Open Figma. Now you're not exploring, you're producing. The structure is settled. The design system work begins.

This is three stages, not two. Most teams skip the middle stage entirely and go from vague idea (whiteboard) to production-quality mockup (Figma). The middle stage — rough but specific, cheap but shareable — is where decisions actually get made.

---

## Why AI Makes This More Important, Not Less

AI tools have dramatically accelerated the production stage of design. You can generate polished Figma components faster than ever. AI copilots in Figma and Framer can turn a rough idea into a high-fidelity screen in minutes.

This is dangerous if your ideation process is broken, because now you can build the wrong thing much faster than before. Speed to production is only valuable if you're producing the right thing.

The counter-move is to invest in the ideation phase. Make the sketching step faster, not slower. Use AI to generate rough spatial ideas quickly — describe in English, get a hand-drawn sketch, share it, discuss it, throw it away, generate another one. Spend 10 minutes in the rough phase and 2 hours in production, instead of 2 hours in the rough Figma phase and 30 minutes wondering if you built the right thing.

---

The best product teams in 2026 aren't the ones with the best Figma skills. They're the ones who've mastered the art of being confidently wrong, quickly.

Rough first. Polish second. Always in that order.
