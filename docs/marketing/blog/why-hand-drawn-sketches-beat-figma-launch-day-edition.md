But there's a specific thing Figma gets wrong that nobody talks about — and understanding it will make you a better designer even if you never touch another sketch tool.

---

## The Feedback Trap

Here's a scenario most product designers have lived through:

You spend three hours in Figma building a mockup. You share it in Slack. The response: "Can you change the button color?" "The font feels off." "Why is there so much whitespace?"

None of these are wrong observations. But they're also not the feedback you needed. You needed: "Does this flow make sense?" and "Are we solving the right problem?"

The mockup answered the wrong question. By polishing it, you invited critique of the polish.

This isn't a user error. It's **fidelity signaling** — a documented phenomenon in UX research. The quality of the artifact signals what kind of feedback is appropriate. A rough sketch invites structural thinking. A polished mockup invites cosmetic critique.

You can't change this by asking people to "focus on the concept." The visual signal overrides the verbal instruction every time.

---

## What Figma is Actually Good At

Figma is exceptional at what it's designed for: **communicating decided design to a build team**.

When you know what you're building, and you need to communicate exact spacing, component states, responsive breakpoints, and interaction flows to engineers — Figma is irreplaceable. It's the right tool for the right job.

The problem is we use it too early. We reach for Figma before we've decided what we're building, because it's the tool we know. And the overhead of a polished tool in an exploratory phase creates two costly failure modes:

1. **You commit too early**: Polishing something makes it feel real. Real things are hard to throw away.
2. **You get the wrong feedback**: Cosmetic critique when you needed structural validation.

## The AI-Native Argument

Here's where this gets interesting for 2026.

AI agents are now the primary authors of first-draft visual output. When Claude draws a floor plan, it's not a designer making aesthetic choices — it's a language model predicting what tokens best describe a spatial layout.

The output should *look* like what it is: a structured first draft.

A photorealistic rendering generated by AI says: "This is how it will look." That's epistemically dishonest at the draft stage.

A hand-drawn sketch says: "This is the structure we're proposing. Tell me what to change."

Skissify's rendering engine uses wobble algorithms — multi-harmonic curves that produce lines with natural variation — specifically to reinforce this signal. The aesthetic isn't a stylistic choice. It's a communication strategy.

## The Practical Workflow That Works

**The Skissify-to-Figma pipeline:**

1. **Describe the concept to Claude** (via Skissify Human Mode or MCP)
2. **Iterate on structure** — "make the kitchen bigger," "add a second bathroom" — until the layout makes sense
3. **Share the sketch link** for structural feedback
4. **Lock the layout** when structure is agreed
5. **Open Figma** and build the polished version

Step 1-4 used to take days. Now they take minutes.

The key insight: don't skip the sketch phase because you're in a hurry. The sketch phase is what prevents the three-week sprint that solves the wrong problem.
