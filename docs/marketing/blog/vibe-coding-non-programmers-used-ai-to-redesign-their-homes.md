## What Homeowners Actually Want

They don't want CAD. They don't want a floor plan at 1:50 scale with precise measurements. They want a sketch — something fast, rough, good enough to answer "does this feel right?"

Hand-drawn sketches are perfect for this. They're immediate. They look like thinking, not deciding. They're easy to discard and replace.

When we launched Skissify, we expected our users to be AI developers. The first unexpected audience to appear was homeowners. Specifically: homeowners using Claude to think through renovation plans who discovered that Skissify let them actually see what Claude was describing.

1. Tell Claude the dimensions and constraints
2. Read Claude's text response describing layout options
3. Try to visualize it mentally
4. Open a room planner app
5. Laboriously re-enter all the information Claude just processed
6. Look at the result
7. Go back to Claude with updates
8. Repeat

**Total time**: 20–45 minutes per iteration.

## Why Hand-Drawn Is Right for This

You might think: "Why not generate a precise floor plan?"

Because precision anchors expectations. When something looks precise, people assume it's decided. They question it less. They offer less feedback.

A hand-drawn sketch signals: this is thinking. It says "here's one way to interpret what you described — is this what you meant?"

That's the right posture for early-stage spatial planning. Homeowners don't need CAD. They need a conversation partner who can draw.
