# The Tuesday Morning Test: Why Rough Sketches Get Better Feedback Than Figma Wireframes

**TL;DR:** The rougher your design looks, the more honest feedback you get. This isn't aesthetic preference — it's psychology. Skissify exploits it on purpose.

---

There's a meeting dynamic every product designer knows but rarely names.

You spend three hours in Figma. Clean auto-layout. Consistent spacing. Proper typography. You walk into the design review, share your screen, and everyone says: "Looks good to me."

You get *no useful feedback*.

The next week, you sketch the same idea on a napkin — or generate a rough floor plan sketch in Skissify — and show it in the same meeting. Suddenly everyone has opinions. "That hallway feels narrow." "What if the kitchen was on this side?" "I thought we agreed the living room faces east?"

Same concept. Same problems. Completely different responses.

---

## Why Polished Designs Kill Honest Feedback

Researchers call this the **fidelity signal effect**. When something looks finished, people unconsciously treat it as finished. They assume decisions have been made. They're afraid to suggest changes because it would "waste all that work."

A rough sketch sends the opposite signal: *this is still being figured out*. It invites revision. It makes people feel like collaborators rather than critics of a fait accompli.

Figma is an extraordinary tool for communicating decisions that have been made. It's genuinely bad at creating the psychological conditions for making decisions *well*.

The napkin sketch phase — the rough, spatial, approximate drawing stage — was AI's missing feature until now.

---

## The Figma Tax

Here's what actually happens when you open Figma to "quickly sketch an idea":

1. Create a new file (name it)
2. Add a frame (what size? Web? Mobile? 1440? Tablet?)
3. Set up a grid
4. Choose whether to use your component library
5. Decide if auto-layout is appropriate for this
6. Import fonts if they're not cached

Twenty minutes later, you're adjusting column spacing and you've completely lost the thread of the *idea* you were trying to capture.

This is the Figma tax. Every minute spent on fidelity is a minute spent *not thinking about whether the idea is right*.

## What Rough Looks Like in Practice

The hand-drawn aesthetic Skissify produces isn't an accident or a style choice. The multi-harmonic wobble algorithm — three overlapping waveforms with variable phase — produces line variation that looks like a careful hand with a good pen.

It *looks* approximate because spatial ideas *are* approximate at the ideation stage.

When you generate a floor plan sketch from a description:
- Walls have natural wobble
- Corners aren't perfectly square
- Dimension lines have slight variation

The result is something that reads as "here's how I'm thinking about this space" rather than "here's what the space will be." That's the right signal to send in a concept conversation.

## The Show, Don't Describe Problem

There's a second problem rough-sketch-first workflows solve: description failure.

Ask someone to "imagine a 4m × 5m kitchen with an island on the north side and a window above the sink." Most people can't hold that accurately in working memory. They'll nod, and then they'll be surprised when the actual space doesn't match what they thought.

A sketch — even a rough one — gives everyone the same reference. You're not aligning on a description; you're aligning on a picture.

That's why homeowners using Skissify to brief contractors have said things like: "He understood exactly what I wanted for the first time."
