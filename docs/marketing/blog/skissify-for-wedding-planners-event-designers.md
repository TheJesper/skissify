We launched Skissify for AI developers.

The pitch was: *"Give your AI agent a pencil. It can now draw floor plans, diagrams, and spatial layouts in hand-drawn style."*

The audience I had in mind: Claude Code users. Mastra builders. MCP experimenters.

The first unexpected group that found us: wedding planners.

## The DM I Didn't Expect

Two days after launch, I got a message. Someone had used Skissify — not through a Claude plugin, not through the MCP server — just through the browser, Human Mode, clicking + dragging shapes.

They were planning a backyard wedding reception for 80 guests.

They needed to figure out table placement, the arc of the ceremony, where to put the bar so it didn't block the dance floor. They tried Google Slides. Too rigid. Tried pencil on paper. Couldn't share it with the caterer. Found Skissify through a Reddit comment and just... started sketching.

"It felt like drawing on a napkin but I could share the link."

---

## Why This Makes Sense (Even Though I Didn't Plan It)

Event planning is fundamentally a spatial problem.

Where do people flow? Where do they gather? Where do things need to be visible from? What's the bottleneck?

These questions don't have precise answers. They need *rough estimates*. Approximate circles where the band goes. A blob for the catering tent. An arrow showing guest flow from parking.

Figma is too precise. A whiteboard gets erased. CAD software requires a degree.

Skissify is the right level of rough for this kind of thinking.

The hand-drawn aesthetic is a feature here, not just a stylistic choice. A hand-drawn floor plan is *clearly a proposal*, not a blueprint. It invites collaboration. It says "here's a rough idea — what do you think?" instead of "this is the plan."

That psychological difference matters enormously when you're coordinating with a caterer, a photographer, a venue manager, and your future spouse.

## Other Unexpected Sketches We've Seen

Since that first wedding message, I've been paying attention. Here's what people are actually drawing with Skissify that I didn't design for:

**Garden layouts.** Where to put the raised beds, the shed, the seating area. Particularly popular for people planning spring projects.

**Party room setups.** Kids' birthday parties, graduation dinners, office holiday parties. Where does the projector go? Can we fit a bounce house?

**Classroom arrangements.** Teachers sketching desk configurations before rearranging 30 desks. (This one surprised me most. Very practical.)

**Home renovation phases.** Not full floor plans — just "if we knock out this wall, what does the flow look like?"

**Camping trip site layouts.** Which tent near which tree, where the fire pit goes relative to the food area.

## What This Means for Skissify

I built Skissify to give AI agents a visual output format. That's still the core.

But Human Mode — the simple drag-and-drop interface we shipped for non-developers — turns out to solve a problem that millions of people have: *I need to think spatially, I don't need perfection, and I need to share it.*

The AI developer use case is deep. The unexpected human use case might be wide.

Both matter.
