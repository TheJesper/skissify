## The Hidden Tax of Polished Tools

Here's what nobody tells you about Figma (or Sketch, or any high-fidelity tool):

**The interface assumes you already know what you're building.**

Every affordance in Figma nudges you toward precision. Alignment guides. Smart pixel-snapping. Auto-layout constraints that need configuration. A component library that looks wrong if you don't use it correctly.

These features exist because *finishing* a design requires precision. But *starting* a design requires the opposite: permission to be wrong.

A rough sketch gives you that permission. Figma quietly takes it away.

## What Actually Happens in Early Ideation

Research on design cognition (see: Suwa & Tversky, 1997; Goldschmidt, 2017) consistently shows that rough, ambiguous sketches support better ideation than detailed mockups.

Why? Because ambiguity invites completion.

When a collaborator sees a polished Figma frame with precise typography and branded colors, their brain reads it as *finished*. Feedback shifts from "what should this do?" to "I'd move that button three pixels left."

When they see a wobbly rectangle labeled "main content" and a squiggly line marked "navigation," their brain reads it as *in progress*. Feedback shifts to the thing that actually matters: does this idea make sense?

This is called **the fidelity signal** — the visual polish of an artifact communicates how open to change it is.

Rough = open. Polished = closed.

You want rough at 10 PM before a launch.

## The Paradox of Restraint

The best design tool for early-stage work is the one that *can't* be polished.

That's why sticky notes still work in design sprints. That's why whiteboard sketches unlock better conversations than slidedecks. That's why a Sharpie on paper still beats a stylus on an iPad for early ideation.

The restraint is the feature.

Skissify makes rough sketches programmatic. You can generate them from text, from JSON, from an AI agent running in Claude or Cursor. And they look like someone drew them with care but without Figma — which is exactly the signal you want to send at 10 PM the night before.
