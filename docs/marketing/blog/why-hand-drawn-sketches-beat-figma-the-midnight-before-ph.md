---

## The thing Figma gets wrong

Figma is an incredible product. This is not a Figma-is-bad post. I use Figma. Most of the design world uses Figma. It is objectively excellent at what it does.

What it does is help teams communicate *decisions that have already been made*.

A Figma screen is a proposal. It says: this is real, this is what we're building, give me precise feedback. The UI chrome, the spacing tokens, the component instances — they all signal "this is serious." 

That signal is a trap in the early stages of any project.

---

## The fidelity problem

When you show someone a polished mockup, they give you polished feedback. They argue about button colors. They ask about font choices. They want to know if that's the real icon or a placeholder.

When you show someone a rough sketch, they tell you if the idea makes sense.

That's the gap. Figma produces the wrong kind of feedback at the wrong time.

The homeowner who emailed her contractor didn't need a Figma mockup of her kitchen layout. She needed something that said "here's what I'm thinking" in a language her contractor could respond to with "yes" or "that wall is load-bearing."

The dungeon master generating maps didn't need pixel-perfect hexes. He needed something that looked like a dungeon — wobbly walls, uncertain scale, pencil-over-graph-paper energy — because that's what sparked the imagination of his players.

---

## Why this is the right moment

We're five days into launch week. Tonight at midnight, we go live on Product Hunt. Tomorrow (April 1, yes) is the day.

The thing I keep thinking about is this: **every AI tool that launched this year produces polished output.** Crisp vector icons. Perfect paragraph spacing. Smooth gradients. Studio-quality voice.

Skissify deliberately produces rough output. 

Not because we can't do polished. Because rough is the point.

The wobble algorithm — the thing that makes our sketches look hand-drawn — is the most deliberate design decision in the product. We tuned it for weeks. We benchmarked 200+ AI-generated sketches to see what amplitude of waviness made something feel "sketched" rather than "broken."

We landed on this: **a sketch that looks hand-drawn lowers the cognitive cost of giving feedback.** It signals iteration. It says "this is not finished, and that is intentional."

---

## The hierarchy of fidelity

Here's the mental model I've settled on:

1. **Whiteboard sketch** — you're still in problem space
2. **Hand-drawn sketch** (Skissify) — you have a shape, you're exploring solutions
3. **Digital wireframe** — you have a solution, you're testing it
4. **Figma mockup** — you have an answer, you're communicating it
5. **Prototype/staging** — you're about to ship it

Most tools serve the top of that list or the bottom. Nothing has really served steps 1-2 well for AI-agent workflows.

That's the gap Skissify fills.
