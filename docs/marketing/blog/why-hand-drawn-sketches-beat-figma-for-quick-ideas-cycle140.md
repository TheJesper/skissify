# Why Hand-Drawn Sketches Beat Figma for Quick Ideas (The Sunk Cost Problem)

*Posted: April 4, 2026 — Cycle 140*

---

There is a reason bad ideas survive design reviews. It is not because they are good. It is because someone made them look good first.

The sunk cost wireframe is one of the most underacknowledged failure modes in product development. You open Figma. You spend 45 minutes building something. It looks polished. You add a shadow here, align the grid there, choose a typography size that feels right. Then you walk into the review meeting — and you have already spent too much time to honestly want it torn apart.

So has everyone else in the room.

---

## What Happens After You Make It Look Good

Polished artifacts create invisible social contracts. When something looks finished, the unspoken signal is: *this is the direction, we are refining now.* The reviewer shifts from evaluator to editor. Instead of questioning the layout, they correct the spacing. Instead of challenging the information architecture, they suggest a different button label.

This is not laziness. It is a deeply human response to perceived effort. We do not want to be the person who says "this whole thing is wrong" after someone spent an afternoon building it.

Rough sketches break the contract. A hand-drawn box with a wobbly line is obviously a placeholder. There is nothing to protect. The social cost of contradiction drops to zero.

---

## The Research Behind Rough

This effect has a name in design psychology: **perceived completeness bias**. Studies on prototype fidelity show that the more finished an artifact appears, the more feedback it receives about surface characteristics (color, spacing, typography) rather than structure (flow, hierarchy, user goals).

This is why Balsamiq became a $10M+ business in the 2010s. They were selling permission to be uncertain. The low-fidelity aesthetic was the product, not a limitation.

## The Two-Meeting Pattern

The teams that iterate fastest use two types of design sessions, not one:

**Session 1: Rough** — Hand-drawn sketch, 10 minutes of prep, no visual polish. The question is: *is this the right structure?*

**Session 2: Precise** — Figma, full wireframe, component library. The question is: *is this the right execution?*

The failure mode is collapsing both sessions into one — building in Figma when you should still be sketching. You pay twice: once in Figma hours, and once in the structural mistakes that survive because no one wanted to say they were wrong.

## The Decision Guide: Figma vs Skissify

Use Skissify when:
- You are testing whether a layout direction is right
- You need a non-precious artifact that invites disagreement
- An AI agent needs to show its spatial reasoning
- You have less than 5 minutes before a meeting
- You want feedback on structure, not surface

Use Figma when:
- Structure is confirmed, execution is the question
- You need pixel-perfect specs for developers
- You are handing off to a component library
- Brand consistency matters for this artifact
- The meeting audience is non-technical stakeholders expecting polish

The best teams use both. The mistake is using Figma first.
