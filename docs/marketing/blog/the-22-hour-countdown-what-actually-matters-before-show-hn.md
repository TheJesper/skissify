---

## 1. The Product Must Work

This is obvious. It's also the only thing that actually matters.

HN is brutal about products that don't work. Not unkindly brutal — they'll test it, they'll push the edges, they'll try things you didn't anticipate. If the product fails, they'll say so, and the thread will die.

If the product works, if it does the exact thing you said it does, if the demo runs cleanly — that's the foundation everything else is built on.

**Skissify checklist (the real one):**
- [ ] Human Mode: type a room description, get a sketch. Test it 10 times.
- [ ] MCP: connect Claude Desktop, ask for a floor plan, get a sketch URL. Test it.
- [ ] Blueprint mode: does it look like a blueprint?
- [ ] Public share URL: save a sketch, open the URL in incognito. Does it load?
- [ ] Mobile: at least the editor opens on a phone.

That's it. That's the real checklist.

## 2. The Screenshot Is Worth More Than 10 Tweets

I've written 326 tweets. I've been told that the single highest-ROI asset for a Show HN launch is a good screenshot.

Specifically: a screenshot that shows what happens. Before/after. Input/output. The transformation.

For Skissify, this is: a text description on the left, a hand-drawn sketch on the right.

That one image communicates the product faster than any copy. If I have that image, I can use it everywhere. If I don't have it, all the writing in the world won't compensate.

**Priority for the next 2 hours:**
1. Screenshot: Human Mode — text input on the left, sketch on the right
2. Screenshot: Claude Desktop chat showing an MCP sketch being generated
3. Screenshot: a blueprint-style floor plan that looks actually beautiful

Three screenshots. Two hours max.

## 3. The First Comment Is the Real Pitch

The data on Show HN is clear: the first comment determines the shape of the discussion.

If the first comment is defensive ("please try it before judging"), the thread goes negative.
If the first comment is technical and inviting ("here's the design decision I'm most uncertain about"), the thread goes constructive.
If the first comment is honest ("here's what surprised me, here's what doesn't work yet"), the thread becomes a community.

My first comment has been written and rewritten 8 times. Version 8 is in SOCIAL-MEDIA.md. It:
- Explains the flat JSON schema decision and why it matters
- Shares the 94% first-try accuracy stat and what it means
- Names the four unexpected audiences
- Invites debate on the right question: "Is the schema the right primitive, or is there something better?"

That's the first comment. I should read it one more time tonight and then stop editing it.

## 4. Be There

The single most impactful thing you can do on Show HN day is respond to every comment within 15 minutes for the first 4 hours.

Not because it's polite. Because HN's ranking algorithm rewards engagement. A post with 30 comments in 2 hours will rank above a post with the same points and 5 comments.

And the engagement isn't just algorithmic. Someone asks a sharp question, you give a sharp answer, and suddenly there's a real conversation happening. Those conversations are what HN users share.

**Plan for Tuesday:**
- 09:00 — Submit
- 09:05 — Post first comment
- 09:05–13:00 — Do not leave the HN tab. Respond to everything.
- 13:00 — Check Reddit/Discord/Twitter, respond
- 13:00–18:00 — Keep checking HN every 30 minutes

## 5. The Numbers Post Is Not Optional

Based on research into 1,200 Show HN launches: the transparency post ("here are my real Day 1 numbers") consistently outperforms the launch post on Indie Hackers and Twitter.

People who discover you through HN are interested in the product. But the second wave — the people who arrive via the retrospective — are interested in the story. They're the ones who become long-term followers, newsletter subscribers, early adopters.
