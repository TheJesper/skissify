## The Real Problem with Figma in Early Design

Figma is a precision instrument. The moment you open a blank file, you're confronted with decisions: canvas size, color styles, grid settings, component libraries, naming conventions. You're one click away from entering production design mode.

But early-stage thinking isn't production. It's **exploration**.

The hidden cost of Figma-first design is this: **when something looks polished, people react to polish, not ideas.** They nitpick typefaces instead of questioning whether the page should exist at all. They adjust padding instead of asking if the feature solves the right problem. They optimize details instead of interrogating the concept.

A hand-drawn sketch removes that trap entirely.

"Is this the right thing to build?" is the only question a sketch can answer. That's why sketches work.

## The Cognitive Science Case

Research in design cognition has established this pattern repeatedly: low-fidelity prototypes generate qualitatively better feedback from stakeholders and team members than high-fidelity ones.

This isn't just about the artifact — it's about what the artifact signals.

A pencil sketch signals: *this is an idea, not a decision.* It invites pushback. It welcomes "what if we moved the kitchen here instead?" A Figma mockup, even if clearly labeled "rough", signals effort expended. People become reluctant to throw it away, even when they should.

The imperfection of a hand-drawn sketch is a **feature**, not a bug. The wobble on a line says "this is approximate." The hand-lettered label says "we can change anything." That is exactly the psychological frame you want at the start of a project.

## The Speed Argument

Here's the second problem with Figma-first design: it's genuinely slow.

Even with the best component library, drawing a floor plan or product layout in Figma takes minutes. Drawing it properly takes hours. And then you're emotionally invested in it.

A napkin sketch takes 30 seconds. A rough floor plan: a minute. You can draw five versions in the time it takes to open Figma's layer panel.

Speed at the exploration stage is not about being lazy. Speed is what allows you to **explore more ideas** before committing. It's what lets you try the weird option that might be brilliant before defaulting to the safe choice.

## Where Figma Wins (and Should)

To be completely fair: there are clear, irreducible jobs for Figma.

- **Handoff to development**: Figma's inspect panel and dev mode are unmatched.
- **Reusable component systems**: Design tokens, shared styles, component libraries — Figma manages this at scale.
- **Real-time collaborative design**: When multiple designers need to work on the same file simultaneously, Figma is the standard.
- **Client presentations**: When you need polished artifacts for external stakeholders.

None of these are "quick idea" jobs. They're all delivery-phase activities. Figma is correct for them.

The argument isn't "replace Figma." The argument is: **don't start with Figma.** Sketch first. Figma second.

## The Workflow That Works

Here's how the most effective designers I've seen use both tools:

1. **Minutes 0-5**: Sketch the concept. Pen, whiteboard, or Claude + Skissify. No constraints. Multiple variants if you have time. Get the idea out of your head and into a form that other people can see.
2. **Minutes 5-20**: Talk about the sketch. What's right? What's wrong? What's missing? What would change if we moved this? This conversation is what you paid for.
3. **After alignment**: Only then, open Figma. Now you're not exploring — you're documenting and refining an idea you've already validated.

The sketch is the thinking. Figma is the finishing.

## The Three Cases Where This Matters Most

**1. Early client/stakeholder meetings**  
Show a sketch before showing a mockup. It invites the client into the design process instead of turning them into a reviewer. You want clients saying "I was thinking more like this" — not "can we change the blue?" A sketch gets you the first reaction. A mockup gets you reactions to the mockup.

**2. Internal architectural decisions**  
When debating where to put what — whether it's rooms in a house, sections of a product, or modules in a system — rough diagrams outperform polished ones every time. The rough version is easier to tear apart.

**3. AI-assisted iteration loops**  
With Skissify + Claude, you can generate five layout variants in the time it used to take to open Figma.
