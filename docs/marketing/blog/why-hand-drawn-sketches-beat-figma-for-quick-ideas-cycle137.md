# Why Hand-Drawn Sketches Beat Figma for Quick Ideas (The Product Manager's Shortest Argument)

---

Here is the fastest argument for sketching before designing:

**The stakeholder who nods at a Figma wireframe and the stakeholder who shrugs at a napkin sketch give you the same information.**

One of them just cost you three hours to produce.

---

## What Figma Costs at the Wrong Moment

Figma is a precision tool. When you open it, you are implicitly committing to precision. The grid snaps. The spacing is exact. The font is Figma's default Inter. Before you've done any design thinking, the thing already *looks like a design*.

That appearance is the problem.

When something looks finished, people evaluate it as finished. A stakeholder who sees a clean Figma wireframe starts asking about whether the button should be blue or gray. They're not questioning the layout. The layout reads as decided.

A sketch is different. A sketch — especially a rough, wobbling, hand-drawn one — communicates: "this is thinking, not deciding." The same stakeholder who skipped over the layout question in Figma will say "wait, shouldn't that sidebar be on the right?" when looking at a sketch.

That feedback is more valuable than the button color conversation.

---

## The Cognitive Science in One Paragraph

Research on design communication consistently shows a phenomenon called the "completion bias" — people are less willing to critique artifacts that appear complete, because criticism feels like vandalism of finished work. Rough sketches trigger the opposite response: incompleteness is an invitation. The brain pattern-matches "rough" to "in progress" and switches into contribution mode instead of evaluation mode.

This is not a nice-to-have. It is the mechanism by which early-stage design feedback is generated or suppressed.

You can fight it with careful prompting ("please give me honest feedback, imagine this isn't finished"). Or you can just start with a sketch.

## The Disposable Artifact Principle

The best thing about a Skissify sketch is how little you lose if you throw it away.

If you've spent forty minutes on a Figma wireframe, you're emotionally and temporally invested in it. Feedback that requires a major structural change feels costly.

If you've spent eight seconds generating a sketch from a sentence, "let me redo this" is not a problem. It's the same effort as writing another sentence.

Disposability is not a weakness. For quick ideas, disposability is the feature.

## When to Use What

**Use Figma for:**
- Communicating final designs to engineers
- Creating the source of truth for production UI
- Stakeholder sign-off on visual language (color, typography, spacing)
- High-fidelity prototypes for usability testing

**Use Skissify for:**
- Resolving layout questions before committing to a design
- Creating spatial context for AI coding prompts
- Stakeholder feedback on structure without triggering the completion bias
- Anything that will change three more times before it's real

The two tools are not competing. They're sequential. Sketch first, Figma second — in that order, for that reason.
