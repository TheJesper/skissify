# Why Hand-Drawn Sketches Beat Figma for Quick Ideas (The Monday Argument)

*Publish: Monday March 30 — Medium, LinkedIn Pulse, Dev.to*  
*Read time: 3 minutes*  
*Audience: Product managers, designers, founders*

---

There's a phenomenon in design every PM knows but almost nobody talks about.

You show someone a polished Figma mockup. They say "looks great!" and you lose the next 6 hours to feedback about button radius and font weights.

You show someone a rough sketch on a napkin. They say "wait, should this even work this way?" — and you have the conversation you actually needed.

The fidelity of the artifact sets the expectations for the feedback.

## Why roughness is a feature

When something looks finished, people assume it *is* finished. Their feedback calibrates to details. They're not going to tell you the whole concept is wrong — that ship has sailed.

When something looks rough — hand-drawn, imperfect, clearly in-progress — people engage with the idea underneath. They question the structure. They suggest alternatives. They co-own the problem.

This is the paradox at the heart of early design: the more polished your artifact, the less useful the feedback you'll get.

## Figma has a polish problem (for ideation)

Figma is an incredible tool. For shipping production UI, for design systems, for handoff — it's unmatched.

But at the ideation stage, it's actively harmful.

Getting to a presentable Figma frame takes time. That time creates attachment. You've invested effort; the people looking at it assume that effort means it's close to final. The social contract of polished work is: "tell me what's wrong with the details, not the whole thing."

Hand-drawn sketches break that contract intentionally.

A sketch says: *this is a placeholder for a conversation, not a proposal for a decision.*

That's exactly what you want at ideation stage.

## What changed with AI

Until recently, hand-drawn sketches required either:
1. A person with spatial confidence (many people have drawing anxiety)  
2. A whiteboard session that lives and dies in the room

AI changed this.

AI agents can describe spatial layouts with precision. They can generate structured JSON that encodes room relationships, object positions, and architectural logic. They do this reliably.

## The contractor test

Here's the practical version:

Show a contractor a Figma mockup of your kitchen renovation. They'll focus on whether your appliance icons are to scale.

Show them a hand-drawn sketch of the same kitchen. They'll say: "this island is going to block traffic — move it 50cm north."

The second conversation is the one that saves you from a €15,000 mistake.

Roughness removes the noise. You get to the signal faster.

## The cognitive science version

There's actual research behind this. Studies of design review sessions show:

- High-fidelity prototypes attract **detail-level** feedback
- Low-fidelity prototypes attract **concept-level** feedback
- Early-stage concept-level feedback has **4–6x** more impact on final product quality

Figma is designed to produce high-fidelity artifacts. Skissify is designed to produce low-fidelity artifacts on purpose.

Different tools. Different jobs. Different moments.

## When to use each

| Stage | Tool | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| "What should this even be?" | Hand-drawn sketch | Invites concept feedback |
| "Does this flow make sense?" | Low-fi wireframe or sketch | Structure over style |
| "Is this ready to ship?" | Figma | Pixel-level precision |
| "Show me the final thing" | Figma | Production handoff |

Skissify lives in the first two rows. It was never meant to replace Figma. It was meant to happen *before* Figma — at the stage where the idea is still moldable.
