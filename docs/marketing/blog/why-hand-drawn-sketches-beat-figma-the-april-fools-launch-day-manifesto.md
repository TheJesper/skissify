## The Research Nobody Talks About

Cognitive science has a name for this: **premature convergence**. When your medium looks finished, your brain treats it as finished. High-fidelity mockups close off exploration before it starts.

The 1990s MIT Media Lab knew this. The 2010s UX research confirmed it. And yet, in 2026, we still open polished tools for 2-minute ideas.

The irony: the less your sketch looks like a design, the more useful feedback you get.

Why? Because when something looks rough:
- Stakeholders fix ideas instead of fixing pixels
- You question assumptions instead of defending choices
- The drawing is a *question*, not a *statement*

## The Three Minute Test

Here's a challenge I gave to 12 PMs last month:

> "You have 3 minutes. Sketch the login flow."

Half opened Figma. Half grabbed a marker.

**Figma group after 3 minutes:** 1 screen. Colors TBD. Fonts placeholder.
**Marker group after 3 minutes:** 4 screens. 3 alternative flows. 1 already crossed out.

The marker group explored 4× more ideas in the same time.

Skissify is the digital version of the marker group.

## What "Hand-Drawn" Actually Means

Most tools fake it. They slap a texture filter on clean vectors and call it sketchy.

Skissify does something different: the hand-drawn aesthetic is **computed, not filtered**.

Every line is rendered using multi-harmonic sine waves with seeded randomness. The seed is deterministic — same JSON, same sketch, every time — but the character varies by element. A door arc wobbles differently than a wall. A furniture item has different line weight than a room boundary.

The result doesn't look like someone tried to make something look hand-drawn. It looks like someone actually drew it.
