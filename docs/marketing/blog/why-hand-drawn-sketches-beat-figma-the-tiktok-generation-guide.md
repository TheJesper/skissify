**That's not a Figma problem. It's a signal problem.**

---

## The Science Behind Why Polish Kills Early Feedback

Research from Stanford's d.school documents a consistent phenomenon: when people view high-fidelity prototypes, they enter *evaluation mode*. They critique and find flaws.

Show them a rough sketch? They enter *collaboration mode*. They add ideas. They say "what if."

The polish of your artifact determines the kind of thinking it invites.

This isn't a bug in how people think. It's actually adaptive - polished things usually *are* meant for evaluation. The problem is when you show polished work at the ideation stage: you're accidentally signaling "this is done" when you mean "what do you think?"

The roughness of a hand-drawn sketch signals: **we are still in the thinking phase.** 

That signal alone changes the conversation.

---

## Three Scenarios Where Figma Actively Hurts You

### 1. Early client presentations

A pixel-perfect mockup in client meeting #1 has two predictable outcomes:
- They love it and resist changes because "you already built it"
- They hate the specifics (wrong blue) and forget to engage with the concept

A rough sketch forces them to engage with the idea, not the execution.

### 2. Sprint kickoffs with remote teams

Async teams need something they can react to quickly. A Figma file requires access, the right version, sometimes plugins. A sketch URL you can paste anywhere and everyone sees the same thing.

### 3. AI agent design reviews

Here's the modern problem: AI agents can now generate interfaces, floor plans, spatial layouts, diagrams mid-workflow. They've been doing it in text.

Text is the wrong format for spatial thinking. 

"The living room is 5x4 meters on the south side of the apartment" is less useful than:

```
┌──────────────┐
│  living room │
│    5x4m      │
└──────────────┘
```

Even *that* ASCII sketch communicates faster.
