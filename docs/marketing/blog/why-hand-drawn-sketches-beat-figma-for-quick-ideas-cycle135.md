# Why Hand-Drawn Sketches Beat Figma for Quick Ideas

*Cycle 135 — April 5, 2026 — Angle: "The $0 Feedback Trick" — how rough sketches extract honest opinions that polished mockups kill*

---

There is a specific moment in every design review where the feedback stops being useful.

You know the moment. The slide goes up. Everyone nods. Someone says "looks good." Another person says "maybe the button could be slightly bigger?" The meeting ends. Nothing changed — not because the design was perfect, but because **polished visuals suppress honest reactions**.

Skissify exists because this problem is worth solving. And the solution is embarrassingly simple: present a rough sketch instead.

## The Politeness Tax

Here is a real dynamic no one talks about openly:

When a design looks finished, disagreeing with it feels rude. People intuitively calculate the effort that went into it. A pixel-perfect Figma frame with proper shadows and brand colors signals: someone spent days on this. Criticizing it feels like attacking their work.

A rough sketch signals the opposite: *this took ten minutes and I want your help making it better.* The implicit social cost of disagreement is zero.

This is why early-stage founders who sketch on napkins get better product feedback than teams presenting polished decks. It's not a design philosophy. It's applied psychology.

## The Specificity Problem in Figma

There's a second, less obvious issue.

Figma forces specificity before you're ready for it. Open a new Figma file and the first thing it asks is: what size? Desktop? Mobile? Tablet? You pick one. Now you're committed.

Next: font. Figma has one loaded. You use it or you spend three minutes browsing Google Fonts when you should be thinking about whether this feature makes sense at all.

Each micro-decision in Figma narrows the idea space. By the time you have something to show, you've made thirty small choices that weren't the point — and everyone in the review will react to those choices, not to the underlying concept.

A hand-drawn sketch defers every one of those decisions. The proportions are approximate. The colors are absent. The interaction states are implied, not built. This is not a limitation. It is the point.

## In 2026, This Gap Is Wider Than Ever

AI coding tools have compressed execution time dramatically. A Claude agent can scaffold a production-grade feature in four hours. An AI design agent can generate a polished Figma file in three minutes.

What hasn't changed: the cost of executing the wrong idea.

When building is fast, the decision of *what to build* becomes the bottleneck. And that decision lives in the rough, uncertain, exploratory phase — the phase where hand-drawn sketches are the right medium and Figma is the wrong one.

There's a term for this that's becoming common in AI-native teams: the **napkin phase**. It's the window between "we have an idea" and "we're committed to executing it." It should last as long as it needs to. Figma closes it prematurely because it makes ideas look real before they are.

## A Simple Rule: Match Fidelity to Certainty

When you know what you're building, use Figma. Its precision is a feature when you need precision.

When you're still figuring out what you're building, use a sketch. Its imprecision is a feature when you need flexibility.

Most teams violate this rule chronically. They jump to Figma the moment they have any idea at all, trading exploration time for execution time at a terrible exchange rate.
