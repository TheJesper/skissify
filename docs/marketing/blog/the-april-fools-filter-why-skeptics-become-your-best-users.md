# The April Fools Filter: Why Skeptics Become Your Best Users

*Published: April 2, 2026 | Author: Jesper | Platform: IndieHackers, Medium*

---

We launched Skissify on April 1, 2026.

Not by accident. Not out of laziness. By deliberate design.

And I want to explain why — because the reasoning changed how I think about cold traffic, user intent, and what "qualified" means when you're a solo founder with zero ad budget.

---

## The Setup

Skissify is a sketch tool for AI agents. You send JSON, you get a hand-drawn sketch URL back. MCP native. Free tier. Works in Claude Desktop with four lines of config.

We started building in late March. The Product Hunt slot opened up. April 1 was available.

The natural instinct: pick April 2. Or April 3. Something safe.

The decision we made: April 1 on purpose.

---

## The Filter Theory

Here's the marketing insight that made us keep the date:

**Every person who sees "April 1" and clicks anyway has already self-filtered.**

They didn't ignore it. They didn't scroll past. They stopped, registered the skepticism, and clicked through to find out if it was real.

That's not passive traffic. That's active curiosity.

And active curiosity is the highest-quality pre-qualification you can get from cold traffic.

Compare these two users:

**User A** arrives on a normal Tuesday after clicking a "check this out" link in a Slack message. No specific intent. Maybe they'll try the demo, maybe they'll close the tab.

**User B** arrives on April 1 after actively deciding "wait, is this a joke?" They've already engaged mentally. They're in a higher-attention state. They want to know the answer.

User B is better. Every time.

---

## What Actually Happened

The skepticism → curiosity → genuine use pattern was real.

The first comments on Product Hunt split cleanly:
- "Is this an April Fools joke?" — then they tried the demo and answered their own question
- "Okay this is actually real" — posted within minutes of trying it
- "Launched on April 1 and the product is genuinely useful, nice" — that's the ideal reaction

The engaged users — the ones who asked product questions, posted sketches they'd made, came back the next day — had a higher rate of "was initially skeptical" than our normal traffic.

I don't have clean A/B data on this. I'm a solo founder with a SQLite database and a cron job, not a growth team with Mixpanel. But the qualitative pattern was loud enough to feel like signal.

---

## The Counterargument

The thing April Fools costs you: press.

Journalists have a built-in filter for April 1. They won't cover a launch on that date because they can't trust the timing. You lose newsletter coverage, you lose journalist pickups, you lose the "launched today" news hook.

That cost is real.

But for a bootstrapped solo product with no PR budget, press coverage isn't your acquisition channel anyway. Your acquisition channels are:
- Communities (Reddit, Discord, Hacker News)
- Social (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Bluesky)
- Word of mouth from users who actually tried it

All three of those work differently on April 1. Communities are **more active** — people are scanning for pranks, which means they're scrolling more than usual. Social is **noisier** but the signal that cuts through is something that's genuinely not a joke. Word of mouth is **faster** because "I tried this on April Fools and it's actually real" is a more memorable story than "I tried this on a random Tuesday."

---

## The Wobble Amplifier

There's a secondary effect I didn't anticipate.

Skissify's sketches use wobbly lines. That's intentional — the wobble signals "draft" to human readers, which changes how they respond to the output (they critique structure, not polish).

But on April 1, the wobbly lines also read as playful. Deliberately imprecise. Aesthetically consistent with a day that's supposed to be funny.

The product and the launch date had visual and tonal coherence. That's hard to engineer. We got it by accident.

---

## The Framework (If You Want to Apply It)

Not every product should launch on April 1. But the filter theory generalizes.

**Any constraint you put on your launch that requires users to opt through will raise the average quality of the users who opt through.**

- Launch at midnight instead of 9am → filters for your most dedicated audience
- Launch with a waitlist → filters for commitment
- Launch with no landing page, just a demo link → filters for people who'll actually try it
- Launch on April 1 → filters for people with active curiosity

The cost of each filter is reach. The benefit is intent.

When you're a solo founder with no marketing budget, you can't outspend reach. You can outdesign intent.

---

## What I'd Do Differently

One thing: I should have written this post before the launch, not after it.

The frame — "April Fools as a user quality filter" — is a marketing argument that would have traveled well before launch day. It would have pre-framed the audience. It would have turned the date into a story instead of a risk.

Instead I wrote it after. Which is fine. But if you're planning a launch with a similar constraint, lead with the argument. Make the unusual choice the headline, not the footnote.

## The TLDR

We launched Skissify on April 1 because every person who clicks through "is this real?" has already self-qualified as curious.

Curious users are better users.

The April Fools constraint didn't hurt us. It filtered for the audience we wanted.
